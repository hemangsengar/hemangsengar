Most engineers pick a lane: research or product, code or content, hustle or peace. I never got that memo.

I write software the way the old texts describe **karma yoga**: full effort on the work, no attachment to the applause.

> **कर्मण्येवाधिकारस्ते मा फलेषु कदाचन**
> *"Your right is to the work alone, never to its fruits."*
> That line is from the Bhagavad Gita. My translation for engineers: ship the thing, ignore the vanity metrics, iterate.

## 🪔 The Human Behind the Commits

A few things that don't fit on a resume but explain everything else:

- **I learn by running things in production.** reciprocateyou is my longest-running project, and it taught me payments, ops, customer support, and the difference between code that compiles and code that people depend on.
- **I read the ancients to debug the moderns.** Vedic philosophy and behavioral psychology are my off-hours stack. A 5,000-year-old framework for detachment from outcomes turns out to be excellent engineering advice.
- **Slow living, fast shipping.** These are not contradictions. Deep work needs a quiet mind, and a quiet mind makes better architecture decisions.
- **I automate what bores me** so I can obsess over what doesn't. Hence the homelab, the workflow automations, and the multi-agent code-audit experiments.

**Ask me about:** RAG that actually retrieves the right thing · voice agents under 1s latency · taking a product from idea to real users · why the Gita is a systems-design book in disguise
